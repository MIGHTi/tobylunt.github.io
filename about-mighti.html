A little about us

What if you could feed people and animals a high quality protein that requires less water, fewer resources, and minimal labor compared to traditional livestock? At MIGHTi, we believe insects are the solution. Yes, that’s right… farming insects. The edible insect industry as well as the practice of entomophagy (eating insects) is growing globally – and we aim to be leaders in promoting rural insect farming in food insecure regions by supporting women’s cooperatives with the training and resources they need to raise insects. Learn more about the project by reading our concept note, or exploring this website.

Concept Note

Our Commitments

Community-Driven

We have learned that the most effective, transparent and lasting changes come from within a community - NOT from outside of it. Consequently, we are partnering with women's cooperatives that want to lead the development of insect farming for themselves and their community.

Environmentally Responsible

A major driver for our work is to reduce society's impact on climate change and environmental degradation, in addition to empowering and uplifting smallholder farmers. Environmental impact assessment is a key tenet of our efforts.

Lasting Changes

Much like the need for change to be driven by community members, it is essential for any development work to plan for the longevity of the work beyond program completion. We will work with our partners to ensure that the work that we do creates change that will long outlive MIGHTi's involvement.

MIGHTi Project Aims

We, as MIGHTi, seek to investigate the potential for women’s cooperatives and schools in rural communities to raise and sell insects (such as mealworms) to boost protein intake, improve food security, and supplement household income.

  • Culturally Tailored Methods
  • Context Specific Microlivestock Kits
  • Environmentally and Economically Sound
  • Year Round Livelihoods and Nutrition

About MIGHTi

MIGHTi is a collaborative research project designed to address the multifaceted and systematic factors that contribute to food insecurity in Southern Africa while simultaneously joining a global effort to re-envision food systems in a changing climate.
